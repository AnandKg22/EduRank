# Company Mission

## 1. Purpose
Defines the absolute core reason the company and product exist. It acts as the ultimate filter for product features, ensuring all engineering and design efforts push in a unified direction.

## 2. Why It Matters
When engineers or AI agents face a crossroad (e.g., "Should we build an admin dashboard for parents or enhance the student battle arena?"), the Mission provides the tie-breaker. It prevents feature bloat and keeps the product lean and focused.

## 3. Example Structure
- **Core Mission Statement**: One clear, powerful sentence.
- **What We Do**: The practical application of the mission.
- **What We Don't Do**: Explicit anti-goals to prevent scope creep.

## 4. Example Content
**Mission**: "To gamify academic mastery and make competitive learning as thrilling as esports."
**What We Do**: Build real-time, high-stakes PvP environments where students prove their academic prowess against peers.
**What We Don't Do**: We do not build traditional, static LMS coursework, video lectures, or passive learning tools.

## 5. AI Usage Instructions
> [!NOTE]  
> If prompted by a user to design a feature that falls under "What We Don't Do", the AI must gently challenge the prompt: 
> "This feature seems to deviate from the core mission of gamifying academic mastery. Should we instead approach this by...?"

## 6. Developer Usage Instructions
- Read this during onboarding.
- If you feel a task you are working on does not serve the mission, raise it with your PM.

## 7. Best Practices
- **Do**: Keep it simple. A mission statement longer than a paragraph is a business plan, not a mission.
- **Don't**: Dilute the mission to appeal to everyone. Be specific about who you serve.

## 8. Maintenance Strategy
- **Owner**: CEO / Founders.
- **Update Frequency**: Rarely (every 3-5 years).
- **Trigger**: Only upon a massive corporate pivot or acquisition.
